Broad Market View

Indian equity benchmarks continued to trade in a narrow range this week, with the Nifty 50 oscillating between ~ INR 25,900–26,300 and the Sensex similarly range-bound, finishing modestly higher over the period. Volatility remained elevated as foreign institutional investor (FII) flows swung between buying and selling, and investors rotated between sectors.

Mid-cap and small-cap indices again underperformed large caps, with more defensive segments leading gains. Continued consolidation around key technical levels suggests markets are seeking fresh catalysts amid mixed global cues and awaited macro data releases.

Theme of the Week: Earnings & Macro Growth Outlook – India Set to Outperform

Indian markets are buoyed by resilient macroeconomic fundamentals and an upbeat earnings backdrop, even as global growth softens. Corporate earnings expectations for Indian equities remain constructive, with consensus forecasts pointing to mid-teens growth in profit pools for the 2025/26 period, driven by stronger domestic demand and cost efficiencies. On the macro front, India’s GDP outlook was recently upgraded, with the economy now expected to expand around 7.4% in FY26, up from ~6.5% in the prior year. This is a notable acceleration and well above the global average.

This growth is powered by robust services and manufacturing activity, solid private consumption, and continued investment momentum, providing a supportive backdrop for corporate toplines and investor confidence.

*Sources : MSCI India earnings outlook consensus, MoSPI / ET India GDP estimates, RBI/ADB growth projections

Expert's Opinion

Q&A with Ajay Khandelwal, Fund Manager at Motilal Oswal Mutual Fund

Q: When you are looking at small-cap stocks that both screen well for growth and valuation, what factors typically tip the balance for you??

For me, the health of the balance sheet is the absolute must-have criterion. In small caps, if working capital becomes stretched or there is a mismatch between capital expenditure and cash flows, you can see massive drawdowns of 50% to 70%

Once the balance sheet is vetted, I look at management differentiation, particularly how they reinvest cash to ensure compounding. I also prefer more liquid, larger market-cap companies within the small-cap space because it makes it easier to build and liquidate positions once upside targets are met. I consciously avoid companies with heavy dependence on a single client, product, or geography.

Q: what is the single biggest risk for a long-term investor looking to increase small-cap exposure right now?

The biggest risk is running out of patience. Small caps are inherently volatile; we've seen several instances in the last 20 years where drawdowns exceeded 20%. Investors often try to time the market after a negative year and end up missing the subsequent sharp recovery.

You have to understand that while not every year will deliver 20-30% returns, the current macro and micro environment is stable, and after a negative year, the probability of performance is often in your favour.

*Source : Small Caps, Volatility and Patience - Ajay Khandelwal explains his approach
